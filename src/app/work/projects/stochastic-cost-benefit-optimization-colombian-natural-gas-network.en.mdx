## Overview

This research presents a **stochastic cost-benefit optimization** framework for the **Colombian natural gas network**, designed to transition national energy planning from traditional deterministic methods toward a **topographically accurate decision-support system**.

The study addresses critical gaps in current planning by the Energy and Mining Planning Unit (UPME), which often neglects **stochastic dynamics**, topographic realism, and the simultaneous operational uncertainty of supply and transportation pipelines.

## Methodology

The proposed approach integrates **altimetric profiles**, spatial topology, and **spatio-temporal failure forecasting** using machine learning to predict future incidents based on historical trends and external variables.

Key components include:

- **System Representation:** Updating georeferenced network data with topographic and topological characteristics to ensure a physically coherent representation of pipeline dynamics.
- **Deterministic Planning:** Establishing a base-case optimization benchmark for production, transportation, and supply decisions under normal operating conditions.
- **Risk and Failure Modeling:** Using **unsupervised learning** to uncover latent failure patterns and **supervised models** to forecast future failures based on historical data from entities such as TGI and ANLA.
- **Decision Support:** Executing **Monte Carlo-based $N-1$ simulations** to perform probabilistic assessments of system performance under disruption scenarios.

## Outcome

The optimization model is re-evaluated under stochastic conditions to quantify the cost implications of supply shortages and rationing, determining optimal production and transport capacities to improve **reliability and energy security** in the national system.
